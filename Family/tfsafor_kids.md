# Can Kids Have TFSAs? Understanding Tax-Free Savings Accounts for Children

By The Editors

Updated May 15, 2025

---

Tax-Free Savings Accounts are powerful savings tools available to Canadians of all ages, including children. Understanding how TFSAs work for kids helps families take advantage of these accounts for long-term savings. This guide covers everything families need to know about children's TFSAs.

A TFSA can be opened for a child of any age, including infants. The child must be a Canadian resident and have a valid Social Insurance Number. The account can be opened by the child if they are an adult (18 or 19, depending on province) or by a parent or guardian on behalf of a minor. This early start provides maximum time for tax-free growth.

Contribution room accumulates from age 18, regardless of when the account was opened. The cumulative contribution limit is tracked by the Canada Revenue Agency. If a TFSA is opened for a child, contributions cannot be made until the child reaches the age of majority. The contribution room that accumulates in the meantime is not available until that time.

The annual TFSA contribution limit applies to all account holders. For 2025, the annual limit is $7,000. This limit applies regardless of age. Family members can contribute to a child's TFSA, but the total cannot exceed the annual limit. The contribution room belongs to the child, not the contributor.

Investment options within children's TFSAs are similar to adult accounts. Cash, guaranteed investments, mutual funds, ETFs, and individual stocks can all be held in the account. The investment choice affects growth and risk. Age-appropriate investment strategies work for children's accounts.

The tax treatment of TFSAs is the same for children and adults. Growth in the account is tax-free. Withdrawals are tax-free. This provides powerful tax advantages that help savings grow faster. The benefits are particularly powerful given the long time horizon of children's accounts.

Contributions to a child's TFSA can come from any source. Parents, grandparents, other family members, or the child themselves can contribute. The contribution room belongs to the child, so contributors cannot exceed the annual limit. Tracking contributions prevents over-contributing.

Trust structures may be relevant for TFSA contributions. Contributions made by adults to a child's account may be considered trusts for tax purposes. The income attribution rules may affect taxation. These rules are complex and professional advice is valuable.

Opening a TFSA for a child requires proper documentation. The child needs a Social Insurance Number. The parent or guardian needs identification. The application process is similar to adult accounts. Most financial institutions allow TFSA opening for minors.

The child can manage the TFSA once they reach the age of majority. Until then, the parent or guardian typically manages the account. The child takes over at the age of majority. This transition should include education about the account.

Registered Education Savings Plans may provide more benefit for education savings. RESPs include government grants that significantly boost savings. The grant matching makes RESPs more valuable for education than TFSAs. However, TFSAs also work for education costs.

Using TFSAs for non-education purposes provides flexibility. Unlike RESPs, TFSAs can be used for any purpose. If a child does not pursue post-secondary education, the money remains available. This flexibility is valuable for uncertain future plans.

Combining TFSAs with other savings vehicles provides comprehensive planning. RESPs for education provide grant benefits. TFSAs provide flexibility and tax-free growth for any purpose. Both can be used together effectively. The combination depends on specific family situations.

Investment strategies for children's TFSAs can be long-term focused. With decades until the funds are needed, aggressive investments may be appropriate. As the child approaches adulthood, investment strategies may shift. The long time horizon allows for recovery from market downturns.

Withdrawals from TFSAs can be made at any time for any purpose. The flexibility makes TFSAs useful for various goals. Withdrawals create contribution room in future years. This feature provides more flexibility than locked-in retirement accounts.

Teaching children about TFSAs provides financial education. Understanding the tax advantages helps children appreciate savings. Involvement in the account builds financial literacy. This education provides value beyond the financial benefits.

Estate planning considerations include TFSA ownership. TFSAs can be included in estates. Understanding the implications helps with planning. Professional advice may be valuable for significant account balances.

Transitioning TFSA management to adult children is an important step. As children become adults, they should understand their accounts. Education about the accounts and their management helps young adults make good decisions. The transition should include clear information about the account.
