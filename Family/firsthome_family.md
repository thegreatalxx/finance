# First Home Buying: Considerations for Families with Children

By The Editors

Updated May 15, 2025

---

Buying a home is a significant decision for any family, particularly when children are involved. The considerations extend beyond simple finances to include family needs, neighborhood suitability, and long-term planning. This guide covers the key factors families should consider when buying their first home.

Affordability is the foundation of home buying decisions. The Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation recommends spending no more than 32% of gross income on housing costs. This includes mortgage payments, property taxes, utilities, and maintenance. Staying within this range leaves room for other family expenses and savings.

Down payment requirements affect how much home you can afford. Canadian mortgage rules require at least 5% down payment for homes under $500,000. Higher down payments reduce monthly costs and may be required for higher-priced homes. The First-Time Home Buyer Incentive and other programs can help with down payment funds.

Location affects family life in many ways. School district quality matters for families with children. Commute time affects family time. Neighborhood safety and amenities affect quality of life. The right location for your family depends on your specific priorities and circumstances.

Space requirements grow with families. Current needs and anticipated future needs should both be considered. Growing families may need extra bedrooms. Work-from-home arrangements may require home offices. Outdoor space may be important for children.

Home type decisions include detached homes, townhouses, and condos. Each has trade-offs of cost, maintenance, and space. Detached homes offer privacy but higher costs. Townhouses provide middle ground. Condos reduce exterior maintenance but have monthly fees.

Child-related features in homes matter for families. Backyard space allows children to play safely. Finished basements provide play areas. Close proximity to parks and schools reduces logistical challenges. These features add to family quality of life beyond the home itself.

Renovation potential affects long-term value. Homes that can be updated and expanded provide flexibility. Outgrowing a home quickly means moving again, with significant transaction costs. Choosing homes with room to grow provides long-term value.

The housing market varies significantly by location. In competitive markets, bidding wars may drive prices up. Understanding local market conditions helps in making offers. Working with experienced real estate professionals helps navigate the market.

Mortgage options for families include various choices. Fixed-rate mortgages provide payment stability. Variable rates may be lower but have risk. Combining mortgage types may work for some families. Understanding the options helps in making good decisions.

First-time home buyer programs provide benefits. The Home Buyers' Plan allows RRSP withdrawals for down payments. First-time buyer incentives may provide grants or shared equity loans. These programs require specific conditions. Researching available programs helps maximize benefits.

Budgeting for ongoing costs prevents financial stress. Property taxes, maintenance, utilities, and insurance add to monthly costs. These ongoing costs should be factored into affordability. The purchase price is only part of the cost of home ownership.

Emergency repairs and maintenance require ongoing budgeting. Homes require regular maintenance and occasional repairs. Setting aside funds for these expenses prevents financial surprises. The rule of 1% of home value annually for maintenance is a useful guideline.

School and child care considerations affect location choices. The quality and proximity of schools matters for families with children. Child care availability and cost affect family finances. These factors should be part of location decisions.

Resale considerations matter even for first-time buyers. Family needs may change, requiring a future move. Choosing homes with broad appeal helps maintain resale value. Location and property characteristics affect future marketability.

The home buying process for families with children involves additional considerations. Showing homes with children present can be challenging. Finding homes that meet family needs requires patience. The process takes longer for families with specific requirements.

Working with real estate professionals experienced with families helps navigate the process. They understand family needs and can identify appropriate properties. Their expertise is particularly valuable in competitive markets. The investment in professional help pays off in better outcomes.
