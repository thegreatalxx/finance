# Asking for a Raise: Timing and Approach

By The Editors

Updated May 15, 2025

---

Asking for a raise is a significant career moment that many Canadians find intimidating. Yet salary increases rarely happen automatically, and those who ask successfully often receive significantly more over their careers. Understanding when to ask and how to approach the conversation dramatically improves your chances of success.

Performance documentation supports any raise request. Tracking your accomplishments, completed projects, and contributions to the organization provides evidence of your value. Specific examples of how you helped the company are more persuasive than general statements. Documentation demonstrates that your request is based on reality, not just your perception.

Timing your request appropriately matters significantly. The best times include during annual performance reviews, after completing a significant project, when taking on additional responsibilities, or when the company is performing well. Avoiding requests during company difficulties, restructuring, or immediately after negative feedback improves your chances. Choose a moment when your employer is most likely to be receptive.

Understanding company processes helps frame your request. Many companies have formal processes for salary increases that happen at specific times of year. Knowing these processes helps you time your request appropriately. If a formal process exists, following it and submitting your request during the appropriate window is important. Working within company systems shows you understand how things operate.

Researching appropriate compensation provides a realistic target. Understanding what similar positions pay in your industry, location, and company size helps you request a reasonable amount. This research provides justification for your request. Being informed demonstrates professionalism and makes your request credible.

Framing your request around value you bring rather than personal needs is more effective. Employers are more convinced by arguments about your contributions than your financial pressures. Focus on what you have achieved, what you bring to the role, and how you have grown. This approach aligns your interests with the company's interests.

Preparing for the conversation thoroughly improves your chances. Practice what you will say, anticipate questions, and prepare responses to common objections. Being prepared helps you appear confident and professional. The conversation may take unexpected directions, but being ready for key points helps you stay on track.

Asking directly is more effective than hinting. State clearly that you are asking for a salary increase and be specific about the amount you are requesting. Ambiguity leads to ambiguous responses. Making a clear request provides a specific thing for your employer to consider and respond to.

Being prepared to discuss timing is important. Your employer may not be able to respond immediately or may need to get approval. Ask when you can expect a response. This shows you are serious and gives you a framework for following up. The timeline might not be exactly what you hoped, but you will have clarity.

Handling a "no" response gracefully preserves the relationship. If your request is denied, ask for feedback on what you need to do to earn an increase. This shows maturity and gives you specific things to work on. Understanding the company's perspective helps you improve your chances in the future. Even a no can be valuable information.

Following up appropriately after the conversation demonstrates professionalism. If you asked for a specific timeline, follow up at that time. If your situation changes, such as taking on new responsibilities, revisit the conversation. The follow-up shows you are serious and keeps the issue active.

Building a case for future raises creates ongoing opportunity. Documenting your accomplishments regularly, not just when preparing to ask for a raise, builds a strong foundation. Each success contributes to future requests. This ongoing documentation makes future conversations easier and more successful.

Alternatives to salary increases may be available if salary increases are not possible. Additional vacation time, flexible scheduling, professional development opportunities, or one-time bonuses provide value. These alternatives can be valuable, particularly when salary increases are constrained. Being open to alternatives shows flexibility and keeps the relationship positive.

Multiple approaches over time may be necessary. Rarely does one conversation result in the desired outcome. Building a pattern of success over time leads to significant compensation growth. Each conversation builds toward your ultimate compensation goals. Persistence, combined with strong performance, leads to results.

Confidence in your request improves outcomes. Believing you are worth more makes you more persuasive. While you should be realistic, believing in your value shows in how you communicate. Employers respond to confidence and can sense when someone truly believes they deserve more. Project the confidence you want to feel.
