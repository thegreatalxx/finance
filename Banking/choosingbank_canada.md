# Choosing a Bank in Canada: Big Five Banks vs Online Banks

By The Editors

Updated May 15, 2025

---

Selecting the right bank is one of the most important financial decisions Canadians make. The banking landscape in Canada is dominated by the large institutions, but online banks and credit unions offer compelling alternatives. Understanding the differences between these options allows you to choose a banking partner that aligns with your needs, preferences, and financial goals.

The Big Five banks in Canada are Royal Bank of Canada, Toronto-Dominion Bank, Bank of Montreal, Scotiabank, and Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce. Together, these institutions hold the majority of Canadian banking assets and operate the largest branch networks. Their ubiquity means you can find a branch or ATM nearly everywhere in Canada, making them convenient for those who prefer in-person banking. However, this convenience comes with costs that may not be obvious at first glance.

Branch access represents a significant advantage of the Big Five banks. Having physical locations available for transactions, advice, and problem resolution matters to many customers. Complex banking situations, such as managing multiple accounts, handling significant transactions, or dealing with disputes, may be easier to resolve in person. The ability to walk into a branch and speak with someone directly provides a sense of security that some customers value.

The fees charged by major banks often exceed those of online alternatives. Monthly account fees can range from $10 to $30 per month for basic chequing accounts, with additional charges for transactions beyond included limits. While these fees may seem manageable individually, they add up significantly over time. Many customers pay hundreds of dollars annually in banking fees without realizing the total cost.

Online banks, sometimes called direct banks or digital banks, operate without physical branches. Institutions like Tangerine, Simplii Financial, and others operate entirely online, with customers accessing services through websites and mobile apps. This model allows these institutions to offer lower fees and higher interest rates on savings because they do not bear the cost of maintaining branch networks. The savings are passed to customers in the form of better pricing.

Interest rates differ significantly between traditional banks and online alternatives. Online banks typically offer higher interest rates on savings accounts, sometimes several times the rates offered by major banks. High-interest savings accounts from online banks can provide meaningful returns on cash while maintaining easy access. The difference in interest earned can be substantial, particularly for larger balances.

The technology offerings of online banks are often more advanced than those of traditional institutions. Mobile apps from online banks frequently offer modern interfaces, robust budgeting tools, and innovative features. These banks were built with technology as a core competency, resulting in digital experiences that often feel more current than those of legacy institutions. Tech-savvy customers often prefer these platforms.

However, online banks have limitations that matter for some customers. Complex transactions, such as large cash deposits, certified cheque purchases, or safe deposit box access, require alternative arrangements. Some customers simply prefer the option to visit a branch when issues arise. The lack of in-person support may be problematic for those who value human interaction for certain banking activities.

Credit unions represent another alternative to the major banks. These member-owned institutions operate as non-profits and often provide more personalized service. Credit union fees are typically lower, and profits are returned to members in the form of better rates and services. However, credit union branch networks are more limited, and membership may be restricted based on location, employment, or other criteria.

The Big Five banks often provide comprehensive financial services beyond basic banking. Investment services, mortgage lending, insurance, and business banking are typically available within the same institution. This one-stop-shopping approach appeals to customers who prefer managing their finances in one place. However, the convenience may come with higher costs than using multiple specialized providers.

Customer service quality varies within and between banking categories. Both traditional banks and online banks receive customer complaints about service quality. In-person branch experiences may be better or worse depending on the specific location and staff. Online banks typically offer customer service by phone, chat, and email, but the experience varies. Researching customer satisfaction can provide useful guidance.

The decision between traditional and online banking depends on your specific needs and preferences. If you value branch access, complex transaction support, and in-person relationships, a traditional bank may be appropriate. If you prioritize lower fees, higher interest rates, and modern technology, an online bank may be better. Many Canadians use both, maintaining primary accounts at one institution while using online banks for specific products.

Security is a common concern with online banking, but federal deposit insurance protects funds regardless of the institution type. The Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation protects deposits up to $100,000 per institution. Both traditional banks and online banks participate in this protection. Online banks employ robust security measures, often comparable to or exceeding those of traditional institutions.

Switching banks involves some effort but can be worthwhile when the benefits exceed the costs. The convenience of staying with your current institution may not justify the fees and lower interest rates you pay. Evaluating your banking needs and comparing options periodically ensures you are getting the best value. The banking market is competitive, and alternatives exist for those willing to explore them.
