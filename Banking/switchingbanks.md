# How to Switch Banks in Canada: A Step-by-Step Guide

By The Editors

Updated May 15, 2025

---

Switching banks can feel like a significant undertaking, but modern tools and banking regulations make it easier than ever. Whether you are looking for lower fees, better interest rates, or improved service, changing banks can provide meaningful benefits. This guide walks through the process step by step, making bank switching straightforward and manageable.

Research is the essential first step before switching banks. Understanding what you want from a new bank helps you choose the right institution. Consider your priorities: lower fees, better interest rates, more convenience, better technology, or improved service. Research various institutions, including digital banks, credit unions, and traditional banks. Comparison websites and reviews provide useful information about customer experiences.

Comparing account options requires looking beyond headline features. Consider monthly fees, transaction limits, interest rates, and other features. Some accounts offer promotional rates that may change. Understanding the full terms helps in making comparisons. Requesting account agreements and fee schedules provides detailed information for comparison. The time invested in research prevents regret later.

Once you have selected a new bank, opening an account is typically simple. Many banks allow complete online account opening, requiring identification verification and basic information. Some accounts require minimum deposits to open. The new account can often be opened before closing the old one, allowing for a transition period. Having both accounts simultaneously provides time to move everything over properly.

Listing all automatic transactions and recurring payments ensures nothing is missed. This includes payroll deposits, pension payments, government benefits, bill payments, subscription services, and transfer arrangements. Creating a comprehensive list prevents issues when you close the old account. The list also helps with setting up new arrangements at the new bank.

Direct deposit changes require contacting your employer, pension administrator, or government agency. Many employers allow direct deposit changes through online portals. Others require paper forms that take time to process. Starting this process early ensures your income arrives at the new account promptly. Government benefits can be changed through Service Canada or My Service Canada Account.

Moving bill payments requires either setting up new payment arrangements or transferring existing ones. Many companies allow easy payment method changes through their websites. Some recurring payments can be transferred by providing new account information to the payee. For others, you may need to cancel the old arrangement and set up a new one. Keeping both accounts active during the transition prevents missed payments.

Closing old accounts requires careful attention. Ensure all pending transactions have cleared, including any outstanding cheques. Verify that all automatic payments have been redirected. Request confirmation that the account has been closed and obtain written confirmation. Some banks charge fees for closing accounts shortly after opening, so timing matters. Requesting a final statement ensures you understand any final charges.

Transferring automated payments is a common source of difficulty. Some recurring payments, particularly from government or employers, can take time to change. Maintaining the old account with a small balance during the transition catches any missed transitions. This backup ensures no interruptions while everything is being moved. After confirming all automatic arrangements work, the old account can be closed.

Moving investments held at your old bank requires attention. Registered accounts like RRSPs and TFSAs can often be transferred directly to the new institution without tax consequences. The process typically takes a few weeks and may involve some paperwork. Understanding transfer procedures and any associated fees helps in planning. Some institutions offer transfer incentives that make moving more attractive.

Moving safety deposit boxes requires coordination with both institutions. The contents need to be physically moved, which might require scheduling. New safety deposit box arrangements might be needed at the new bank. Some institutions allow account holders to keep boxes for a period after closing other services, providing transition time.

Customer service during the transition helps address any issues that arise. Both the new and old banks should be able to assist with questions. Keeping records of all communications helps if problems develop. Most transitions proceed smoothly, but being prepared for issues helps address them quickly. Patience during the transition prevents frustration.

The timeline for switching depends on the complexity of your arrangements. Simple accounts might be fully moved within a week or two. More complex situations, particularly with investments and automatic deposits, might take a month or more. Building in adequate time prevents pressure and mistakes. The extra time is a small investment for the benefits of better banking.

Banking research suggests that most Canadians stay with their initial bank for years without evaluating alternatives. This inertia costs money in the form of higher fees and lower interest. Periodically evaluating your banking relationships, perhaps annually or when your situation changes, ensures you continue to get good value. Switching banks is easier than many assume, with the primary barrier being inertia.

Post-switching verification confirms everything works correctly. Monitoring both accounts during the transition catches any issues promptly. Confirming that interest is credited correctly at the new bank. Ensuring all scheduled payments go through properly. The verification period confirms the switch was successful and identifies any remaining items to address. With verification complete, you can fully enjoy the benefits of your new banking relationship.
